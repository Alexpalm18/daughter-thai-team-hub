What Is Wine?

Wine is an alcoholic drink made from fermented grape juice. If a label just says "wine," it's made from grapes. Key fact: wine is made from fruit, beer is made from grains.

The year on the label = the vintage — the year the grapes were harvested and made into wine. Each grape harvest happens once per year.

5 Characteristics of Wine

🍬 Sweetness

From residual sugar (RS) — sugar left after fermentation. Ranges from bone-dry to very sweet.

⚡ Acidity

Natural acids in grapes (tartaric, malic). High acidity = refreshing & tart. Cooler climates = higher acidity.

🌿 Tannin

From grape skins, seeds & oak barrels. Creates a drying, grippy sensation. Mainly in red wines.

🔥 Alcohol

Created when yeast eats grape sugar. Typical range: 10–15% ABV. Higher sugar in grapes = higher ABV.

⚖️ Body

The weight/fullness of wine in your mouth. Light → Medium → Full. Higher alcohol & tannin = fuller body.

Sweetness Scale

Bone Dry Dry Off-Dry Semi-Sweet Sweet

"Dry" = no perceptible sweetness (less than 0.2% residual sugar). Most table wines are dry. Riesling can be dry or sweet — always check!

How to Taste Wine (SEE · SWIRL · SNIFF · SIP)

1

SEE 👁️

Hold glass to the light. Check color, clarity, and viscosity. Deeper color in reds = riper grapes or longer skin contact. White wine with golden hue = oaked or aged.

2

SWIRL 🌀

Swirl gently to oxygenate. Watch the "legs" (tears) running down the glass — thick, slow legs = more sugar or alcohol. This also releases the aromas.

3

SNIFF 👃

Stick your nose in and inhale. Note intensity (faint vs. strong) and aromas — fruit, floral, earthy, spice, oak/vanilla. If it smells like wet cardboard = corked wine.

4

SIP 👄

Take a sip and let it coat your mouth. Note sweetness, acidity (makes you salivate), tannin (drying sensation), body, and finish. A long, pleasant finish = quality wine.

Old World vs. New World

🏰 Old World

  • Europe (France, Italy, Spain, Germany, Portugal)
  • Label by region (Bordeaux, Burgundy, Chianti)
  • Lighter body, higher acidity
  • Earthy, mineral, herbal notes
  • Traditional, strict rules

🌎 New World

  • USA, Australia, NZ, Argentina, Chile, South Africa
  • Label by grape variety (Chardonnay, Cab Sauv)
  • Fuller body, higher alcohol
  • Fruit-forward, jammy, oaky
  • Modern, innovative

The 5 Main Types

🍷 Red Wine

Made with red/black grapes — skin, seeds, and stems are included in fermentation, giving red its color, tannin, and structure. Fermented at higher temperatures.

Easy-drinking reds: Merlot, Pinot Noir, Malbec
Bold reds: Cabernet Sauvignon, Syrah/Shiraz, Zinfandel
  • Steak
  • Lamb
  • Pasta
  • Hard cheese
  • Mushrooms

🥂 White Wine

Made from white grapes or red grapes with skins removed. Only the juice is fermented. Results in a crisp, refreshing profile — acidic, creamy, or fruity depending on the grape.

Light whites: Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio, Riesling
Full whites: Chardonnay, Viognier, Sémillon
  • Seafood
  • Fish
  • Chicken
  • Salads
  • Soft cheese
  • Thai food ✓

🌸 Rosé Wine

Red grapes fermented with skin contact for only 12–36 hours (just enough to pick up pink color). Light, refreshing, low tannin. Can range from bone dry to slightly sweet.

Common grapes: Pinot Noir, Grenache, Sangiovese, Merlot
  • Salads
  • Charcuterie
  • Light pasta
  • Summer dishes

✨ Sparkling Wine

Any wine with CO₂ bubbles. The bubbles come from a second fermentation. They all fall under "sparkling wine" — the name just changes depending on where it's made.

🥂 Champagne
Only from the Champagne region, France. Made from Pinot Noir, Chardonnay & Pinot Meunier. The gold standard.
🇮🇹 Prosecco
From Veneto, Italy. Made from Glera grape. Lighter, fruitier, more affordable. Great for cocktails (Aperol Spritz).
🇪🇸 Cava
From Spain (mainly Catalonia). Made in the same method as Champagne but with local grapes. Dry, toasty, excellent value.
🌍 Sparkling Wine
Everything else — USA, Australia, NZ, etc. Same style, different origin. If it's not from those three regions, it's just called Sparkling Wine.
  • Celebrations
  • Oysters
  • Sushi
  • Fried foods
  • Light starters

🍯 Dessert & Fortified Wine

Dessert wines are made from very ripe, late-harvest grapes — nearly raisined on the vine. Fortified wines have spirits (usually brandy) added to stop fermentation or raise alcohol to 18–22% ABV.

Key styles: Port (Portugal), Sherry (Spain), Madeira (Portugal), Vermouth (Italy), Marsala (Sicily)
  • Desserts
  • Cheese
  • Aperitif
  • Digestif

Champagne Sweetness Levels

💡 Server Knowledge

When guests ask for "dry" Champagne, they mean Brut or Extra Brut. "Extra Dry" is actually slightly sweeter than Brut — counterintuitive but true!

Extra Brut
0 g/L sugar — bone dry
Brut ⭐
<15 g/L — dry, most popular
Extra Sec
12–20 g/L — off-dry
Sec
17–35 g/L — lightly sweet
Demi-Sec
35–50 g/L — medium sweet
Doux
>50 g/L — very sweet

White Grape Varieties

Chardonnay "Queen of White Wine"
White

The world's most popular white grape. Highly influenced by winemaking style. Unoaked = crisp, fresh, green apple. Oaked = buttery, creamy, toasty vanilla. Champagne uses Chardonnay as its primary white grape.

Serving Temperatures

Sparkling / Champagne 38–45°F (3–7°C)
White Wine (light) 45–50°F (7–10°C)
White Wine (full) / Rosé 50–55°F (10–13°C)
Red Wine (light) 55–60°F (13–16°C)
Red Wine (full-bodied) 60–65°F (16–18°C)
💡 Tip

When in doubt — chill it slightly. A wine that's too warm loses its aroma and freshness. You can always warm a red in your hands, but you can't un-warm a white.

Wine & Thai Food Pairing

Spicy Dishes (Curries, Larb, Papaya Salad)

Off-dry or sweet wines balance heat. Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Moscato are your best friends. Avoid high-tannin reds — tannin amplifies spice.

Seafood & Light Dishes

Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Grigio, unoaked Chardonnay. High acidity cuts through richness and complements delicate flavors.

Grilled Meats & Rich Sauces

Cabernet Sauvignon, Malbec, Syrah. Tannin grips fat, and bold flavors stand up to powerful sauces.

Pork & Duck

Pinot Noir, Merlot. Soft tannin complements the delicate meat without overpowering it.

Vegetable-Forward Dishes

Rosé, Sauvignon Blanc, Pinot Gris. Fresh, herbaceous wines mirror the freshness of vegetables.

Guest-Facing Phrases

When they don't know what they want:

"Do you usually prefer something dry or a little sweet? Light or bold? Red or white?" — 3 questions gets you there.

Recommending for Thai food:

"Our dishes have some heat, so I'd suggest something with a touch of sweetness or high acidity — like a Riesling or Sauvignon Blanc — it balances the spice beautifully."

If they ask "is it good?":

Never say "it's good." Instead: "This one is fruit-forward and smooth — very approachable" or "This has a great acidity that pairs well with seafood."

If a wine smells off:

Don't ignore it. Tell your manager — a corked wine smells like wet newspaper or musty basement. The guest shouldn't pay for a bad bottle.
